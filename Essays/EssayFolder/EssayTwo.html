Essay 2: Digital Colonialism

The recent decision by Google to lay off a significant portion of its workforce from its "Core" teams and relocate roles to countries like India and Mexico is exemplary of a broader phenomenon known as digital colonialism. Digital colonialism refers to the exploitation of less economically developed regions by powerful tech corporations from more developed countries. This paradigm affects local economies, labour markets, and technological ecosystems in a lot of ways, particularly for developers like me who live in regions such as South Africa.

Google's move to shift roles to lower-cost locations highlights a core principle of digital colonialism. But for South African developers, this trend presents both opportunities and challenges. On the one hand, there may be increased opportunities for employment as global companies seek cost-efficient talent. On the other hand, the volatility of such job markets can lead to job insecurity and underemployment. Companies may prioritize cost savings over the stability and growth of local workforces, leading to a cyclical pattern of hiring and layoffs that destabilizes local economies. As Michael Kwet argues, "The national e-education initiative is a primary vessel for Silicon Valley corporations to capture South African education, economy, and society" (Kwet, M.2019), suggesting that the pursuit of cost savings by global tech firms can lead to a form of digital colonialism that undermines local development.

Another significant impact of digital colonialism is the phenomenon of talent drain, where skilled developers in South Africa might find themselves compelled to work for foreign companies due to the allure of higher wages and better working conditions. While this can lead to individual economic advancement, it often results in a brain drain, where the local tech ecosystem suffers from a lack of skilled professionals. This outflow of talent can oppose the growth of local tech industries and innovation.

Digital colonialism using Amrute’s article as a framework.

Digital colonialism, as discussed by Sareeta Amrute in her article "Tech Colonialism Today," refers to the ways in which technology and technological systems achieve and reinforce colonial relationships and power structures. This concept is rooted in the historical context of colonialism, where European powers exploited and controlled non-European territories, cultures, and peoples for economic and political gain. Amrute argues that these colonial dynamics continue to shape the development and deployment of digital technologies today.

Amrute identifies several key characteristics of digital colonialism with the first being the Hierarchical Structure. Hierarchical Structure is where powerful corporations and governments control the development and deployment of technologies, while marginalized communities are relegated to secondary roles. As Madhavi Mallapragada notes, "the dominant narratives of innovation and progress are often shaped by the interests and values of powerful corporations and governments, while marginalized communities are relegated to secondary roles”. This hierarchy is reinforced by the ideology of techno-chauvinism, which assumes that a small group of people in Silicon Valley are best equipped to manage and control technological systems.

Another characteristic of digital colonialism is the extraction and exploitation of resources, including data, minerals, and biological knowledge. This extraction is often done without the consent or benefit of the communities from which these resources are taken.

The third characteristic of digital colonialism is Uneven Consequences. The consequences of digital colonialism are felt unevenly, with marginalized communities often bearing the brunt of the negative impacts.

The fourth and final characteristic put forward by Armute is Malevolent Paternalism. Digital colonialism is also marked by malevolent paternalism, where solutions are presented as being for the benefit of marginalized communities but are designed and controlled by those in power. This paternalism is exemplified by the development of technologies that are marketed as solutions to social problems but are designed to reinforce existing power structures.

With Armutes characteristics in mind, I done some research and investigated examples of digital colonialism. A noticeable example where a tech giant's actions have benefited them while potentially hindering marginalized communities is Facebook's deployment of the Free Basics service. Launched under the mask of bringing free internet access to developing countries, Free Basics provided users with access to a limited set of websites, with Facebook prominently included. This initiative expanded Facebook's user base in untapped markets, driving long-term growth in their advertising revenue and strengthening user loyalty. However, for the communities it claimed to help, Free Basics created a "walled garden" that restricted their internet experience to a curated selection of sites, effectively limiting their access to the full spectrum of information and services available online.

The controversy surrounding Free Basics, especially in African countries, highlights the broader issue of net neutrality, as critics argued that the service violated the principle by favouring certain websites over others. "The content does not include some of the important websites that Ghanaians want to look at," (Solon, O. 2017) said Kofi Yeboah, who researched the app in Ghana, noting the absence of popular news websites such as MyJoyOnline and CityFM. This not only stifled local innovation but also disadvantaged smaller, independent content providers who couldn't afford to be part of Facebook's platform. Ultimately, this example underscores how the hierarchical control exerted by tech giants, can result in initiatives that prioritize corporate interests at the expense of equitable access and fairness for marginalized communities.

Relate it to the labour situation in global software development

The labour situation is a critical aspect of digital colonization in the software development industry. Western tech corporations often exploit workers in the Global South, employing them in low-wage, precarious conditions to support their operations. These workers are typically engaged in tasks such as data entry, content moderation, and software testing, which are essential for the functioning of AI systems and digital platforms. As Mark Graham and Jamie Woodcock note, "The digital economy is built on the backs of workers in the Global South who are engaged in precarious and low-wage work, often in conditions that are exploitative and violate basic human rights" (Graham & Woodcock, 2019).

The exploitation of these workers perpetuates economic and social inequalities, as they are paid significantly less than their counterparts in the Global North and lack access to basic labour rights and protections. This situation is impaired by the lack of strong labour unions and the absence of effective labour laws in many countries in the Global South. The corporations further maintain their dominance by controlling the means of production, such as proprietary software and cloud infrastructure, and by extracting valuable data from users without providing adequate compensation or benefits.

I conducted some research to look for specific case studies that can back up my statements, and I found that content moderators in India are often employed by major tech companies like Google and Microsoft to manage the content on their platforms. These workers are responsible for identifying and removing harmful content, such as hate speech and violent images, to ensure a safe and respectful online environment. However, despite their crucial role in maintaining the integrity of these platforms, content moderators in India are often paid very low wages. Many of them earn as little as $2 per hour, which is significantly lower than the wages of their counterparts in the Global North hindering not only the integrity of the market but the local economy as well. As the International Labour Organization notes, "Low pay and wage inequality remain a serious challenge to India's path to achieving decent working conditions and inclusive economic growth" (International Labour Organization, 2011)

The low wages and lack of benefits for content moderators in India are a result of the exploitative labour practices employed by these tech companies. These companies often outsource their content moderation tasks to third-party contractors, who then hire workers in India and other countries in the Global South. This outsourcing allows the tech companies to avoid direct responsibility for the working conditions and wages of their content moderators, while still reaping the benefits of their labour. As a result, content moderators in India are often forced to work long hours in poor working conditions, with limited job security and few opportunities for advancement.

Relate that to the South African software development industry

In recent years, South Africa has witnessed a concerning trend of talented software developers choosing to pursue job opportunities abroad rather than staying within the country. This phenomenon, commonly referred to as "brain drain," poses significant challenges to the growth and competitiveness of the local tech industry. Emigration trends from South Africa have been on the rise, with skilled professionals, including software developers, opting for opportunities in tech hubs like Silicon Valley, New York, or London. Perceived better career prospects, higher salaries, and more opportunities for advancement abroad have fuelled this trend, drawing developers away from the local job market.

Moreover, the allure of a higher quality of life, better infrastructure, and more favourable work-life balance policies in foreign countries also contributes to the decision of South African developers to relocate. Working for international tech companies or startups offers exposure to cutting-edge technologies, diverse projects, and collaborative work environments, enhancing their skills and professional development. This exposure makes them more competitive in the global job market, further incentivizing them to seek opportunities abroad. Consequently, the emigration of skilled software developers exacerbates talent shortages in the South African tech industry, hindering innovation, and economic growth. As Indiran Govender puts it, “Brain drain and the loss of skilled professionals are a major reason for the unsteady growth of the South African economy”. (Govender, I. 2024).

Along with the loss of skilled workers, and the effect on the economy of the country, the presence of multinational tech giants in the South African labour market can overshadow local startups and innovation. These companies often have vast resources that dwarf those of local competitors, making it difficult for smaller entities to thrive. Furthermore, the focus on aligning with global standards and practices set by these companies can stifle local creativity and innovation, as developers may be more inclined to adopt established methods rather than innovate independently.
