How code matters in decolonialism

When thinking about how our code matters in the context of decolonialism, I've identified several aspects of my own coding practice that I plan to incorporate. As someone who aims to design websites for a global audience, I recognize the importance of prioritizing mobile-first design, especially in regions like Africa where the majority of users access the internet through mobile devices. This means ensuring that my applications are optimized for smaller screens, slower internet speeds, and limited data plans, while also considering the specific challenges of mobile networks in these areas, such as high latency and limited bandwidth.

Another important aspect is language and accessibility. Decolonialism emphasizes the need to center marginalized voices and perspectives, which in coding terms means being mindful of the languages I use and the accessibility of my applications. I should consider developing applications that support local languages and dialects, making them more accessible to a broader audience. Additionally, I should ensure that my applications are accessible to people with disabilities, following Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and other accessibility standards.

Data storage and ownership is another area where I need to be mindful. Decolonialism critiques the exploitation of resources, including data, by colonial powers. As a coder, I should prioritize data privacy, security, and transparency, ensuring that users have control over their data and are aware of how it is being used. This includes using secure protocols for data transmission, encrypting sensitive information, and providing users with clear options for data deletion and management.

Cultural sensitivity and representation are also crucial considerations. My code should be culturally sensitive and representative of the diverse communities I serve. This means avoiding cultural appropriation, stereotypes, and biases in my applications. I should use inclusive language, avoid culturally insensitive imagery, and ensure that my applications are free from biases in their algorithms and decision-making processes.

Collaboration and community engagement are also important aspects of decolonial coding practice. I should work closely with local communities, involving them in the development process and prioritizing their needs and perspectives. This includes engaging in participatory design, where users are actively involved in the design and development of applications, and providing opportunities for community members to contribute to the development process.

Reflection on development process

This week, or rather this entire submission, marks a pivotal shift in my approach. I've transcended mere compliance to actually manifesting the visions I've meticulously outlined in my wireframes and style guide. It all commenced with the index page, envisaged as a gateway adorned with four inviting cards or "big buttons," beckoning users to explore the essence of my website.

Initially, I adorned these cards with CSS, crafting them into visual marvels. Yet, I encountered a profound epiphany – CSS, while masterful in its domain of aesthetics, is impotent when it comes to imbuing elements with functionality. This realization served as my induction into the nuanced realm of web languages. CSS, a virtuoso in styling and embellishment, wields no influence over functionality. Enter JavaScript, the orchestrator of interaction and data manipulation, breathing life into static designs.

Armed with newfound knowledge, I embarked on a journey to infuse functionality into my creation. JavaScript became my beacon, guiding me through the labyrinth of event-driven programming. I discovered the power of events – subtle triggers that enable systems to discern user actions, from hovering to clicking and beyond. Leveraging the 'on click' event, I choreographed the symphony of interaction, directing each click to unveil the corresponding card's realm.

Responsive design stands as both a beacon of innovation and a crucible of challenges in the digital landscape. At its core, responsive design champions inclusivity, ensuring seamless user experiences across an ever-expanding array of devices. This adaptability empowers users to engage with content unhindered, irrespective of screen size or device orientation.

Yet, amid its virtues, responsive design poses formidable challenges. The quest for universality demands meticulous planning and execution. Crafting layouts that gracefully adapt to diverse screen dimensions necessitates a delicate balance between design aesthetics and technical feasibility. Achieving this equilibrium demands a keen understanding of fluid grid systems, flexible images, and media queries.

In essence, responsive design is a double-edged sword – a testament to the boundless possibilities of digital innovation, yet a crucible wherein designers are forged by the fires of adaptability and resilience.
