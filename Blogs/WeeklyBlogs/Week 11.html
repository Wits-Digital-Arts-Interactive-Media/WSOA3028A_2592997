Reflection on "Algorithmic Colonization of Africa"

I found Abeba Birhane's work on "Algorithmic Colonization of Africa" to be a thought-provoking and insightful analysis of the similarities between traditional colonialism and the current dominance of Western tech monopolies in Africa. As someone with a keen interest in the intersection of technology, society, and global power dynamics, this paper really resonated with me.

Birhane's central argument that Western tech giants, driven by profit motives, are exploiting Africa in ways that mirror the colonial era is compelling. The examples she provides, such as the lack of relevance of many AI systems to African contexts and the stifling of local innovation, vividly illustrate her point. This aligns with other scholarly perspectives, such as that of (Crawford, 2021), who notes that AI often perpetuates existing inequalities and power imbalances, rather than alleviating them. I was particularly struck by her observation that this algorithmic invasion is simultaneously impoverishing local development while making Africa dependent on Western software and infrastructure (Birhane, 2020).

What I found most valuable about this work is how it challenges us to think critically about the societal impacts of emerging technologies like AI. It would be easy to get caught up in the hype and promise of these tools, but Birhane reminds us that they are not neutral - they embody the values and interests of their creators. This is echoed by (Noble, 2018), who argues that technologies often reinforce the biases of their developers. If those values are misaligned with the needs of African communities, the results can be exploitative and harmful.

At the same time, I appreciate that Birhane is not rejecting technology outright. She acknowledges the potential benefits that access to quality data and AI developments could bring to Africa. Her vision is of a more equitable, community-centred approach to technological development that empowers Africans rather than subjugating them. This call for a participatory and inclusive approach is crucial, as highlighted by (Eubanks, 2018), who advocates for technologies that serve the public good and are developed with community input.

Ultimately, I believe Birhane's work is an important contribution to the growing body of scholarship examining the sociopolitical implications of AI and other emerging technologies. Her concept of "algorithmic colonization" provides a powerful framework for understanding the dynamics at play and the urgent need for a more just and inclusive approach to technological progress.

References:

  • Birhane, A. (2020). Algorithmic Colonization of Africa.
  • Crawford, K. (2021). Atlas of AI: Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence. Yale University Press.
  • Noble, S. U. (2018). Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism. NYU Press.
  • Eubanks, V. (2018). Automating Inequality: How High-Tech Tools Profile, Police, and Punish the Poor. St. Martin's Press.

Weekly Reflection

This week, or rather this whole submission, I’ve decided to actually try and create what I have illustrated in my wireframes and style guide instead of simply creating what is required from me. So, I began with my index page where I planned to have 4 cards or “big buttons” that allowed the user to navigate to the main components of my website. I used a bit of CSS to design my cards, but I quickly found out that CSS scripts cannot be used to record or facilitate any interaction between the user and the website. This was a big realisation for me as this was the time I actually learnt the actual uses of the different languages. CSS is primarily used for styling and decoration however it has no effect on what the elements do functionality wise, and JavaScript is in charge of not only interaction but data manipulation.

With that being said, I went on and made a JavaScript that controlled what the cards did once clicked on. I learnt that not only buttons but almost every element can be given these things called events that allow the system to recognize things like when the mouse is being hovered over an element, when its being clicked on and so many more. I used the on mouse click event to load the corresponding card’s page.
